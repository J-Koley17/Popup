Right to Freedom

Fundamental Rights of the Indian Constitution

Article 19: Protection of 6 Rights

Article 19 guarantees six fundamental freedoms to all citizens: the right to freedom of speech and expression, the right to assemble peacefully, the right to form associations or unions, the right to move freely within India, the right to reside and settle anywhere in the country, and the right to practice any profession or business. This freedom includes expressing views through various means, holding public meetings on public land, forming or not forming groups, and moving within or outside the country. Restrictions exist to prevent violent assemblies, protect tribal areas, and prohibit immoral or dangerous professions.

Article 20: Protection in Respect of Conviction for Offences

Article 20 protects individuals, including foreigners and legal entities, from arbitrary and excessive punishment. It ensures that no one can be convicted of an offense unless it violates a law in force at the time of the act or face a penalty greater than what the law prescribes. It also guarantees that no one can be prosecuted or punished more than once for the same offense and that no one can be forced to be a witness against themselves.

Article 21: Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

Article 21 guarantees that no person, whether a citizen or non-citizen, can be deprived of life or personal liberty except through a procedure established by law. This right encompasses more than just survival; it includes the right to live with dignity and to have a meaningful and complete life.

Article 21(A): Right to Education

Article 21(A) mandates that the State provide free and compulsory education to all children aged six to fourteen years. This makes elementary education a Fundamental Right but does not extend to higher or professional education. The provision was added by the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act of 2002. Prior to this, Article 45 in Part IV of the Constitution addressed free and compulsory education for children.

Article 22: Protection Against Arrest and Detentions

Article 22 provides protections for individuals who are arrested or detained. It covers both punitive detention, which follows trial and conviction, and preventive detention, which occurs without trial. For ordinary law, it guarantees the right to be informed of arrest grounds, consult a lawyer, be presented before a magistrate within 24 hours, and be released if not authorized for further detention. For preventive detention, it limits detention to three months without an advisory board's approval, requires communication of grounds for detention, and allows the detainee to contest the detention order. These protections apply to both citizens and foreigners.
